CSCB20 — Introduction to Databases and Web Applications: Winter 2025

Welcome to CSCB20 Winter 2025!ğŸ˜Š

This course provides a hands-on introduction to databases and web application development. You will gain both theoretical knowledge and practical skills to build a solid foundation in these areas.

What You will Learn:

Databases:

  • Key terminology and practical applications
  • Creating, querying, and updating databases
  • Designing databases using the entity-relationship model

Web Development:

  • Building static and interactive web documents
  • Setting up web servers and generating dynamic, server-side content
  • Developing web applications integrated with databases

We are excited to have you in the course and look forward to helping you develop valuable skills in databases and web development!

Prerequisite: Some experience with Python, Java, or C.

Exclusion: This course may not be taken after - or concurrently with - any C- or D-level CSC course.

How the Course Is Run

Each week, we will meet twice: once for the lecture and once for the tutorial. Both lectures and tutorials will be in person and will not be recorded.

Coursework Breakdown

Assignments (33%)

  • Assignment 1: 8%
  • Assignment 2: 10%
  • Assignment 3: 15%

Assignments will cover material from 2â€“3 weeks and will be posted on Quercus.

Mid-Term (25%)

  • The mid-term exam will be held in person during lecture time.
  • Tentative Date: February 28
  • Duration: 120 minutes

Tutorial Attendance (2%)

  • Tutorials will include collaborative problem-solving and practical demonstrations of concepts covered in class.
  • Attendance will be recorded, and you must attend at least 7 tutorials to earn these marks.
  • Tutorials are not graded, and problems solved during tutorials are for practice only.
  • If you are unable to attend a tutorial, please submit a valid reason.

Final Exam (40%)

  • There will be one in-person final exam for the course.
  • Note: You must score at least 35% on the final exam to pass the course.

Academic Integrity

All work you submit must be your own, and your work must not be submitted by someone else. Plagiarism is considered academic fraud and is taken very seriously. The department uses software to compare programs for evidence of similar code. Please review the Rules and Regulations from the U of T Governing Council, especially the Code of Behaviour on Academic Matters.

Please also see the Office of Student Academic Integrity for more details.

We want you to succeed and are here to help. Please don't copy.

How to Avoid Plagiarism:

  • Never look at another studentâ€™s assignment solution (except for teammates, if collaboration is allowed). This includes solutions on paper, computer screens, or any other medium.
  • Never show your assignment solution to another student. This rule applies to all stages of your work, including drafts and incomplete solutions.
  • If you find code online that solves part or all of an assignment, do not use or submit any part of it. Many academic offences involve students who unknowingly use the same online source.
  • Be cautious with "tutoring services." Some services encourage academic misconduct, leading to university suspensions. If unsure, ask your instructorâ€”we are here to help.
